Theory & Model Explanation

What is a cloudburst?

A cloudburst is an extreme amount of precipitation in a short period of time, often localized over a small area. It can lead to rapid runoff, flash floods, and can trigger landslides on vulnerable slopes.

Why do landslides occur?

Landslides happen when gravitational forces on slope materials overcome resisting forces. Common triggers include:

  • Intense or prolonged rainfall (increases pore pressure and reduces soil shear strength)
  • Steep slopes
  • Loss of vegetation (reduces root reinforcement)
  • Soil saturation and changes in groundwater
  • Earthquakes or human activity (excavation, loading)

Model used in this demo

This demo uses a Random Forest classifier trained on a synthetic dataset (generated for demonstration only). Features included:

  1. rainfall_mm — intensity of the event
  2. antecedent_rain_7d — recent rainfall accumulation
  3. slope_deg — steepness of terrain
  4. soil_moisture — relative soil wetness (0-1)
  5. ndvi — vegetation index (-1 to 1)
  6. dist_river_km — proximity to river (km)

Important: This is a proof-of-concept. For operational systems you should use real historical landslide inventories, high-resolution rainfall and soil data, and conduct proper validation, bias analysis, and uncertainty quantification.

How to improve this system

  • Use real labeled landslide event datasets (satellite-detected or field-surveyed).
  • Integrate high-resolution meteorological nowcasts and radar-derived rainfall.
  • Include geotechnical layers: soil type, depth, permeability.
  • Use spatial models (e.g. geostatistics, convolutional neural nets on raster inputs).
  • Deploy in the cloud with automated data pipelines and monitoring.
